# Governance Action ID  
`gov_action1k02990lhw6wh74t7c6ufw3mqaek9ujtvyan99dj5qv5kvcs7pn8sztttste`

## Summary  
We consider this governance action to be constitutional.

## Rationale Statement  
This proposal is a Treasury Withdrawal Governance Action to withdraw ₳540,750 from the Cardano Treasury in connection with "Dolos by TxPipe: Maintaining Cardano's Lightweight Data Node, Year 2," proposed by TxPipe. This proposal met the 67% support threshold required during the Hydra voting phase of the 2026 Intersect budget process and has proceeded to the submission of an on-chain Treasury Withdrawal Governance Action in accordance with the approved budget process framework. This proposal references the proposal document on IPFS and presents the proposal's title, summary, rationale, justification, and relevant supporting materials. These elements comply with the requirements regarding the format of Governance Actions and sufficient supporting documentation as stipulated in Article 2, Section 6, Paragraphs 1 and 2 of the Cardano Constitution. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 1, the proposal aims to maintain Dolos as a lightweight and resource-efficient gateway to Cardano chain data, with an implementation period of 12 months. It also outlines four quarterly milestones, developer costs of ₳420,000, a contingency fund of ₳105,000, and an Intersect Budget Administration fee of ₳15,750. It also specifies the conditions, calculation method, evaluation period, and settlement date for returning any ADA exceeding the amount necessary to cover the $105,000 project budget to the Cardano Treasury upon project completion. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 2, it discloses that TxPipe received funding through 30 Project Catalyst proposals from Fund 9 through Fund 14, and that it received open-source maintenance funding for Pallas, Dolos, and UTxO RPC through Intersect's 2025 budget cycle. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 3, the proposal confirms that the requested amount of ₳540,750 falls within the ₳350,000,000 Net Change Limit covering Epochs 613 through 713. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 4, the proposal states that audit and oversight costs are included within the overhead applied to this proposal, and that the Intersect Budget Administration fee covers administrative oversight. It also outlines independent oversight by Intersect and a third party with technical expertise, reporting obligations, and milestone-based payment management. Additionally, the supplementary materials state that Intersect will provide Delivery Assurance oversight throughout the contract period, and that Blink Labs will verify milestone completion and deliverable quality through independent reviews. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 5, Intersect is designated as the administrator, and the proposal outlines milestone-based payments and Delivery Assurance oversight throughout the contract period. It also describes a framework in which Intersect manages the Treasury Reserve Smart Contract (TRSC) and the Project-Specific Smart Contract (PSSC), while an Oversight Committee composed of external, independent third-party organizations oversees specific management actions. Regarding Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 6, the proposal outlines a mechanism whereby upon enactment of this governance action, funds will be managed in the TRSC and transferred to the PSSC once the legal agreement is prepared and the vendor is ready. It is stated that the TRSC and PSSC are auditable by the community, cannot be staked with an SPO, and are delegated to the predefined auto-abstain DRep. Therefore, as no clear conflict with the Cardano Constitution has been identified, this proposal is deemed constitutional.

## Precedent Discussion  
None

## Counterargument Discussion  
It is difficult to argue that a structure in which funds for multiple projects are consolidated into the TRSC using a single receiving address is fully consistent with the requirement for "separate accounts" set forth in Article 2, Section 7, Paragraph 6 of the Constitution. In light of this, some of our members deemed the structure unconstitutional, arguing that it could not be considered constitutional.

## Conclusion  
For the reasons stated above, we determine that it is constitutional.
